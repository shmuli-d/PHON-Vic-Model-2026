One Nation & the 2026 Victorian state election

A scenario model · 88 Legislative Assembly seats · majority = 45 · election 28 November 2026 · not a forecast — a "move the inputs and see" tool

How this is built

No state MRP exists, so the baseline is a hybrid: each seat's Labor / Coalition / Greens / independent vote is the actual 2022 result (which keeps the Greens' real inner-Melbourne strongholds and the Liberal/National split), and One Nation's vote is layered on using its federal geographic pattern (from the federal MRP, projected onto state seats via a seat-overlap map), scaled to the current state poll (~24.5%). Then the same preferential-count engine as the federal model runs in all 88 seats. Treat outputs as scenario illustrations, not predictions.

Polling backdrop — parties & leaders

From the latest statewide Roy Morgan poll (fieldwork 22–24 April 2026, n≈1,707; changes vs February 2026).

Where the parties stand

Primary vote: Labor 25.5% (unchanged) · One Nation 24.5% (−2) · Coalition 24% (+2.5) · Greens 13.5% · Independents 8.5% · Other 4%.

Three-party preferred: Labor 44.5% — well ahead of Coalition 28% (+2) and One Nation 27.5% (−2). Once preferences flow, Labor's lead is much larger than the primary vote suggests.

Head-to-head (two-party preferred): Labor 51 v Coalition 49 · Labor 53.5 v One Nation 46.5 · Coalition 59 v One Nation 41 — in a straight choice, voters back the Coalition over One Nation roughly 3:2.

Regional Victoria flips: One Nation leads the primary there (~36%, ahead of Coalition 28, Labor 19.5), and runs the Coalition to a near dead-heat 2PP (Coalition 50.5 v One Nation 49.5).

Where the leaders stand

Better Premier: Opposition Leader Jess Wilson (Lib) 53% (+2) vs Premier Jacinta Allan (ALP) 42% (−0.5); 5% can't say. Wilson leads among both men and women.

Jacinta Allan (Premier) — job approval: approve 32.5% (+2), disapprove 66.5% (−1) — net −34. Two-thirds of Victorians disapprove of her performance.

Jess Wilson (Opposition Leader) — job approval: approve 48.5% (−4.5), disapprove 48.5% (+6) — net 0, an even split.

No leader-approval figure is published for One Nation (it has no equivalent state-leader profile in this poll). The "Better Premier" question is Allan vs Wilson only.

Source: Roy Morgan, Victorian State Voting Intention, 22–24 April 2026. The takeaway for the model below: a deeply unpopular Labor government, a split-rated Liberal opposition leader who nonetheless leads on "Better Premier," and a One Nation vote that is large but that other voters resist on preferences (especially against the Coalition).

3 · What this can and can't tell you

This is a scenario tool, not a forecast. The seat outcome is most sensitive to two assumptions you control in §1 — the Coalition→One Nation preference flow and whether the Coalition would govern with One Nation. With the current defaults (strong swap, combined right bloc), a high enough ON vote tips Victoria toward a right-bloc result; switch either off and the same primary vote leaves Labor the largest party governing in minority, consistent with the polls' three-party-preferred (Labor well ahead).

Known limitations

  • One Nation's distribution is a federal-pattern proxy. State and federal voting differ; the projection captures ON's broad regional/outer-metro shape but not seat-specific state dynamics.
  • Independents are lightly modelled. The two current independents (Ringwood, South Barwon) are protected as incumbent holds — but both are ex-Labor members now sitting as independents, with no 2022 independent primary, so their hold is an incumbency assumption rather than a vote-based estimate. Strong independent challenges in Liberal seats (Hawthorn/Kew/Malvern-type teal runs) are only as strong as the 2022 independent vote in the data.
  • Preference flows are reused from the federal model and only roughly Victoria-calibrated. The Coalition→ON default (70%) reflects the Victorian Liberals' reported plan to preference ON ahead of Labor (The Age, 26 Apr 2026); the final decision is seat-by-seat by a five-person committee after nominations close.
  • The Liberal "preference-ON" backlash isn't modelled. Reporting (and Kos Samaras) warn the same strategy that helps ON in Labor-vs-ON seats could collapse the small-c conservative Liberal vote in inner/middle Melbourne — making Hawthorn, Kew and Malvern more precarious and energising the teals. The model treats Coalition→ON as a clean transfer; it does not capture moderate-Liberal voters defecting in response, so it likely understates losses in the Liberal urban heartland.
  • Upper house excluded. The Legislative Council (40 seats, 8 multi-member regions) is a different system — balance of power there is a separate question.
  • Candidate coverage is uncertain. One Nation's vote surged only recently, leaving limited time to recruit and vet 88 lower-house candidates before a close election — so it may not stand everywhere, and weak or hastily-chosen candidates can underperform the party's polling. The default assumes it contests all 88 seats; the "contests only its 40 strongest seats" toggle models a constrained field, where in the seats it doesn't contest its vote reverts to the other parties (and it can only win where it actually runs).
  • No state MRP. A proper Victorian MRP would replace the federal projection and materially improve seat-level accuracy.

Sources & method. Baseline: 2022 Victorian Legislative Assembly results by district (Narracan filled from the 2023 supplementary). One Nation layer: federal MRP (RedBridge/Accent, May 2026) projected onto state districts via a seat-overlap map, scaled to state polling. State polling: Roy Morgan, Freshwater, DemosAU (early–mid 2026; ON ~21–26%, Labor ~26, Coalition ~24–25). Liberal preferencing of One Nation: The Age, "Victorian Liberals to preference One Nation at state election" (Chip Le Grand, 26 Apr 2026) — default position pending seat-by-seat decision; cf. the SA election where ON cut the Liberals from 16 seats to 5; the Nepean byelection is ON's first significant Victorian test. Boundaries: VEC 2022 (= 2026) district boundaries. Engine: full preferential (instant-runoff) per seat with a composition-based Greens firewall; government formed against the 45-seat majority line. A probabilistic scenario tool conditioned on mid-2026 information, not a prediction.
